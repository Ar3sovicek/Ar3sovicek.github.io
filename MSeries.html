High-performance sports models

M1

The BMW M1, launched in 1978, is a famous sports car and the first from BMW's M division. Designed by Giorgetto Giugiaro, it has a distinctive wedge shape and mid-engine layout. Its 3.5-liter inline-six engine produces 273 horsepower. Initially meant for racing, street versions were made due to homologation rules. The M1 is rare, iconic, and highly valued in BMW's history.

BMW M1 (E26)

1978-1981

Founded in 1972, BMW M initially focused on developing and building race cars. By 1978, all their models were based on existing BMWs. However, the BMW M1 was a game-changer as it was the first built entirely from scratch. Its chassis was crafted at Marchesi, bodywork at T.I.R in Italy, and the interior was assembled by ItalDesign.

BMW, unable to meet the production demands of 400 street-legal units alone, partnered with Lamborghini for the M1, also known as the E26. The mechanical assembly took place in Stuttgart.

The M1 featured a coupe body style and marked BMW's first use of a center-mounted 3.5-liter 6-cylinder engine, generating 277 hp and 243 lb-ft of torque. This layout allowed for further racing modifications. The next BMW with a center-mounted engine would be the modern i8.

Production of the M1 was limited to just 453 units over three years from 1978 to 1981. It remains one of BMW's rarest and most successful race cars.

In 2008, BMW celebrated the M1's 30th anniversary with the M1 Hommage Concept, sparking discussions about a potential modern M1 model.

M2

The BMW M2 is a small, powerful coupe introduced in 2016. It's known for its strong engine, sharp handling, and sporty design. With rear-wheel drive and a choice of manual or automatic transmission, it offers a thrilling driving experience tailored for enthusiasts.

BMW M2 (F87)

2015-2018

The BMW M2 is a small, powerful coupe introduced in 2016. It's known for its strong engine, sharp handling, and sporty design. With rear-wheel drive and a choice of manual or automatic transmission, it offers a thrilling driving experience tailored for enthusiasts.

The BMW M2 debuted at the Mazda Laguna Seca Raceway, showcasing its superior performance. Setting it apart from other 2 Series models were its distinctive bumper with larger air intakes and flared wheel arches, signaling its higher horsepower. A rear spoiler and quad-exhaust system further emphasized its enhanced capabilities.

For a track-focused experience, the M2 offered an optional M-DCT gearbox and utilized brakes sourced from the M3/M4. Under the hood, it featured a twin-scroll turbocharger instead of the M3/M4's twin-turbo setup, resulting in slightly less horsepower but still impressive acceleration from 0 to 100 kph (0-62 mph). Despite not having components like a carbon-fiber driveshaft found in its larger siblings, the M2 was priced more affordably.

Equipped with adaptive suspension, the M2 provided both comfort on the road and agility. Its wider front and rear tracks, facilitated by an aluminum suspension, contributed to enhanced stability compared to the standard 2 Series.

BMW M2 Competition

2018-Present

The M2 was a serious sports car that sparked debates about whether it could outperform the larger M4 on the track. When BMW introduced the Competition version, the question became even more challenging to answer.

BMW has a rich history with sport-compact sedans, starting with the iconic BMW M3 in the '80s. This lineage continued with a full range of M-Power vehicles, where the M2 stood out as the smallest yet potent model.

The M2 Competition featured a redesigned bumper with specific air intakes for engine and brake cooling. Its enlarged black BMW kidney grilles and black side vents on the front fenders added to its aggressive appearance. At the rear, the four black exhausts distinguished it from other M Competition models, complementing its exclusive Hockenheim Silver color option.

Inside, the M2 Competition catered to track enthusiasts with sport-bucket seats and distinctive M2 Competition badges on the seats and door sills. The instrument cluster showcased a black background with red needles and white lettering.

Technically, the M2 Competition boasted a 3.0-liter twin-turbo engine producing 410 hp. It came standard with a 6-speed manual gearbox, with a 7-speed dual-clutch automatic available as an option. An electronically controlled rear differential ensured optimal traction, enhancing its performance credentials.

BMW M2 CS

2019-2023

In 2019, BMW launched the M2 CS, aimed at enhancing the M2's track performance by borrowing technologies from its larger siblings, the M3 and M4, including their engine.

The M2 CS took BMW's acclaimed M2 to a higher level of performance and everyday usability. It retained its mean appearance while gaining more power and improved underpinnings, making it a favorite among enthusiasts worldwide. Unlike many sports cars focused solely on track performance, BMW designed the M2 CS to excel both on the road and on the circuit. Notably, it offered a three-pedal setup, a rarity in modern sports cars.

Externally, the M2 CS featured an aggressive front fascia with enlarged kidney grilles and additional cooling vents beneath the bumper. A distinctive hood vent aided in heat extraction, complemented by a carbon fiber lip and vents on the front fenders and side skirts. At the rear, a carbon fiber spoiler and a diffuser housing four exhaust pipes completed the sporty look.

Inside, drivers enjoyed an Alcantara-covered steering wheel and trim across the dashboard, center console, and door panels. The high-bolstered sports seats offered comfort and support, with rear seating available for short journeys. The analog instrument cluster included large dials for speed and RPM, while the iDrive infotainment screen sat atop the center stack.

Underneath, the M2 CS shared its S55 inline-six engine with the M3 and M4, providing ample power for both spirited driving and daily commutes. The choice of a six-speed manual or seven-speed dual-clutch automatic gearbox ensured versatility in various driving conditions.

BMW M2

2022-2024

BMW surprised many by introducing the second generation of the M2 in fall 2022.

The 2022 M2 continued its tradition by packing an M3/M4 engine into the smaller 2-Series body. It gained 90 more horsepower compared to the 2015 model but also became heavier. BMW made significant exterior changes, including a blacked-out grille, angular front bumper with squared scoops, and muscular flared fenders. At the rear, it featured a squared lower bumper and a duck-tail trunk reminiscent of the E46 M3 CSL.

Inside, the dashboard resembled the 2-Series Coupe but with dual screens and a separate instrument panel. Sport bucket seats added to the performance-oriented interior feel. Under the hood, it shared the turbocharged straight-six engine with the M3/M4, though with less power, and came with a choice of a six-speed manual or an eight-speed automatic gearbox.

BMW surprised enthusiasts by introducing the updated 2025 M2 just a year after its initial launch. Despite expectations for an M2 CS or Competition variant, BMW opted for subtle yet significant upgrades. The front end featured angular air intakes and a kidney grille with a larger M2 badge. At the rear, quad exhausts with black tips were standard.

M3

The BMW M3 is a high-performance sports sedan known for its powerful engines, sharp handling, and aggressive styling. It's popular among enthusiasts for its blend of everyday usability and track-ready performance.

BMW M3 Coupe (E30)

1986-1992

The BMW M3 E30 was the first-ever M3 and a dominant force in touring car racing, claiming over 1500 victories in its class. Since its debut in 1986, the series model quickly became an icon in motorsports.

The BMW E30 M3 was initially built as a race car to meet touring championship regulations, which required at least 5000 units produced annually and shared parts with the race model. BMW adapted the race car for the road despite sales department concerns.

Based on the E30 coupe, the M3 featured distinct C-pillars and a shorter trunk. It had widened front and rear fenders to house new suspension and a broader track. Two evolutions were produced, with one offering adjustable aerodynamics like a front apron extension and rear wing angle.

Inside, it boasted sport-bucket seats and comfort amenities like leather upholstery, an onboard computer, air conditioning, and a manual gearbox. Under the hood was a newly developed 2.3-liter inline-four engine with twin-cam and 16 valves, producing nearly 200 hp. It offered two 5-speed manual options, including a dog-leg variant with different gear ratios.

BMW M3 Coupe (E36)

1992-1998

The 1992 BMW M3 was the first to feature a six-cylinder engine. Unlike the iconic E30 M3, it was offered as a coupe, sedan, and convertible.

The BMW E36 M3 was conceived due to the success of the E30 M3 and the positive reception of the M5. Unlike its predecessor, it featured a more subtle design without flared arches or a prominent rear wing. Instead, it had distinctive bumpers, aerodynamic sills, and unique rear-view mirrors.

Inside, the E36 M3 boasted sport-bucket seats and special upholstery, along with M-badging on the instrument cluster and seats.

In Europe, it offered 286 hp from a 3.0-liter engine, while U.S. versions were detuned to 240 hp due to different fuel regulations. Later, from 1995 onwards, the engine was enlarged to 3.2 liters, producing 321 hp in Europe and maintaining 240 hp in the U.S., albeit with increased torque. Initially paired with a 5-speed manual transmission, post-1995 models received a 6-speed manual, with an optional SMG automated gearbox available for both variants.

BMW M3 Cabriolet (E36)

1994-1999

BMW launched the second generation of the M3 in 1992, but it wasn't until two years later, in 1994, that they unveiled the convertible version of the car.

BMW introduced the third generation of the 3 Series in 1990, but fans had to wait until 1992 for the M3 version. Unlike its predecessor with an inline-four engine, the E36 M3 featured a powerful inline-six. The convertible version arrived in 1994, sporting a similar front design as the M3 Coupe, including a lower air-dam with a center intake and rectangular fog lights. Its aerodynamic package enhanced with sculpted side skirts aimed to reduce drag. At the rear, a black diffuser and dual exhausts added to its sporty appeal, though more subtly than the E30 M3 Convertible.

Inside, the M3 Convertible boasted high-bolstered bucket seats upfront and a curved dashboard hosting an instrument cluster akin to lower-spec 3 Series models. The rear bench accommodated two passengers with individual headrests.

Under the hood, a potent 3.2-liter inline-six engine was paired with a five-speed manual transmission initially. Later, starting with the 1995 model year, BMW introduced significant facelifts and modifications to the E36 lineup.

BMW M3 Sedan (E36)

1994-1998

BMW launched the second generation of the M3 in 1992 as a coupe, and by 1994, they introduced the four-door version.

The second generation of the BMW M3, unlike its predecessor which was only available as a coupe, offered a sedan and convertible option as well. It maintained a more subtle appearance compared to regular models, though distinct details set it apart. The sedan featured a front bumper with a central rectangular air intake and squared fog lamps, while aerodynamic door mirrors and sculptured rocker panels enhanced its profile. At the rear, it sported a diffuser and trunk-mounted wing.

Inside, the M3 sedan boasted a three-spoke steering wheel with an M-badge, high-bolstered bucket seats with optional leather, and standard features like a high-fidelity sound system and onboard computer. Under the hood, it initially housed a 3.0-liter inline-six engine with 286 PS for Europe and 240 horsepower for the U.S., later upgraded in 1995 to a 3.2-liter engine delivering 321 PS in Europe and the same 240 horsepower in the U.S.

BMW M3 Coupe (E46)

2000-2006

BMW launched the third generation of the M3 in 2000, based on the E46 platform, showing that the automaker had learned from past experiences and was continuing this popular version of the 3 Series.

After the successful E30 M3, BMW made significant strides with the E36 generation by introducing inline-six engines. However, it was with the E46 M3 that the model truly evolved. Featuring the renowned S54 inline-six engine, it set new standards in performance.

Unlike its predecessor, the E46 M3 Coupe stood out with a more aggressive front bumper sporting a distinctive V-shaped lower grille and cooling scoops for the brakes. It also included vents on the fenders reminiscent of BMW's historic 507 lineup. The car's profile was enhanced by flared wheel arches and aerodynamically shaped side sills, while the rear boasted quad exhausts and a trunk lip spoiler.

Inside, the M badge was proudly displayed on the steering wheel, instrument cluster tachometer, and manual transmission gear stick. Standard were high-bolstered bucket seats, with sports seats available as an option. The cabin could also be equipped with a satellite navigation system mounted on the center stack.

Under the hood, the E46 M3 housed an exceptional naturally-aspirated inline-six engine delivering over 100 horsepower per liter. It offered a choice between a six-speed manual transmission or BMW's unique SMG (Sequential Manual Gearbox), providing enthusiasts with top-tier performance and driving dynamics.

BMW M3 Cabriolet (E46)

2001-2007

The third generation M3 was available as both a coupe and a convertible. Despite the convertible being heavier than the coupe, it still delivered impressive performance.

The first generation BMW M3 was initially a racing-oriented car adapted for street use to meet FIA approval. Its unexpected commercial success led BMW to continue offering M3 versions for subsequent 3-Series models. The 2001 M3 (E46) marked the third generation. While over 17,000 units of the E30 M3 were produced, the E46 M3 convertible alone sold nearly 30,000 units.

The car's aggressive appearance was characterized by its distinctive "Angel Eyes" rounded parking lights and a large bumper with a lower apron to accommodate radiators and coolers. Hood scoops helped release hot air from the engine bay, and modern vents on the front fenders echoed the design of the historic BMW 507. The A-pillar was reinforced for structural integrity, but no other visible reinforcements were added above the body.

Inside, the M3 Convertible provided seating for four with front bucket seats as standard. Rear space was limited for longer journeys. The soft-top could fully retract behind the rear bench, and the dashboard and center console mirrored those of the coupe version.

Under the hood, the M3 Cabriolet featured the same 3.2-liter S54 engine paired with a standard 6-speed manual transmission. An optional 6-speed automated gearbox called SMGII with a single-clutch system was also available.

BMW M3 CSL (E46)

2003

The 2003 BMW M3 CSL was a special edition model, paying homage to the legendary 1972 BMW 3.0 CSL. The CSL initials stood for "Coupe, Sports, Lightweight," emphasizing its focus on performance and reduced weight compared to the standard M3.

The BMW M3 CSL, named after its “Coupe Sport Leichtbau” characteristics, was meticulously crafted to shed weight and enhance performance. Only produced for a year with just 1383 units, its exterior featured subtle changes like a larger front bumper and a distinctive rear spoiler. Special wheels and small “CSL” badges further distinguished it.

Inside, the focus was on weight reduction with race-style bucket seats replacing heavier ones. Even the rear bench was made lighter and could be easily removed for racing purposes. Under the hood, its 3.2-liter straight-six engine was tuned for more power than the standard M3, paired with a SMG II gearbox and a limited-slip differential. Overall, the CSL model was 110 kg (243 lbs) lighter, enhancing its agility and driving dynamics significantly.

BMW M3 Coupe (E92)

2007-2011

The final naturally aspirated BMW M3 debuted in 2007 with a larger V8 engine. Surprisingly, this new engine was lighter than its predecessor, the six-cylinder it replaced. This change improved sound quality, increased power output, and enhanced handling characteristics.

The E92 BMW M3, launched in 2007, stood out with its distinctive hood bump, fender vents, and lowered suspension, setting it apart from other E92 models. Built to excel as a grand tourer, sports car, and track performer, it featured a potent eight-cylinder engine paired with a six-speed manual transmission. Purists initially had mixed feelings, longing for the original E30 M3 from 1986, which had a smaller, naturally aspirated six-cylinder engine.

Compared to its predecessor, the E46 M3, the E92 was heavier due to its larger engine but compensated with increased torque at lower revs. Inside, the focus was on combining comfort with driving pleasure, featuring stylish leather sport-bucket seats and BMW's classic instrument cluster. An infotainment display and iDrive controller were positioned high on the dashboard, while rear passengers enjoyed decent space.

Technological advancements like new suspension, a standard limited-slip differential, and advanced electronics enabled the E92 M3 to deliver superior speed, cornering ability, and drifting performance compared to its predecessor.

BMW M3 Cabriolet (E93)

2008-2013

The 2007 BMW M3 marked the end of naturally aspirated M3 models and the debut of a V8 engine, except for the rare 2001 BMW M3 GTR edition.

The fourth-generation BMW M3 was offered in sedan, coupe, and convertible versions, known internally as the E93. The convertible featured a retractable hard-top that added weight but enhanced driving enjoyment.

Distinctive features included a unique bumper with large air intakes and a hood bulge accommodating the tall 4.0-liter V8 engine. Functional air vents on the hood and sides cooled the engine and brakes, while four exhausts and a rear diffuser adorned the back. The three-piece hard-top retracted in 22 seconds.

Inside, it offered typical convertible space with improved rear legroom due to a longer wheelbase, though seating was limited to two.

Power came from a 4.0-liter naturally aspirated V8 paired with a 6-speed manual or a 7-speed automated gearbox, the latter offering quicker gear changes for faster acceleration.

BMW M3 Sedan (E90)

2008-2011

The 2007 introduction of the fourth-generation BMW M3, which included the third iteration of the four-door model, set a new benchmark in the sport-sedan market, becoming the car to beat.

The M3 story began in the '80s with a street car modified for FIA approval, starting with the successful E30 M3 available only as a two-door. The next E36 M3 added a four-door variant, which continued in later models alongside coupe and convertible options.

The M3 sedan stood out with a distinct front bumper featuring a larger grille and side air-dams, plus a hood bulge for the larger engine. Side mirrors were new, and the rear boasted four centered exhausts under the bumper.

Inside, Motorsport GmbH colors adorned the upholstery, steering wheel, and instrument cluster, with the M3 logo prominent. Front bucket seats provided exceptional cornering grip.

Underneath, a 4.0-liter V8 engine powered the front wheels through a 6-speed manual or 7-speed dual-clutch automatic gearbox, the latter making the car slightly quicker to 100 kph (62 mph). Its balanced weight distribution ensured impressive cornering speeds.

BMW M3 Coupe (E92) LCI

2010-2013

In 2010, BMW introduced a facelift for the last naturally aspirated V8 M3, adapting to eco-friendly trends without sacrificing power.

In 2010, as the global economy recovered, interest in sport coupes surged. The M3 Coupe, a top choice in its segment, received a mild refresh and special editions. It sported a new black kidney grille and an optional carbon-fiber lip on the front bumper. Inside, subtle changes included 8 LED shift lights on the instrument cluster and relocated heated seat controls. To reduce CO2 emissions, BMW added a start-stop system for both manual and automatic transmissions. Additionally, a Competition Package debuted, featuring adjustable damper settings for track enthusiasts.

BMW M3 GTS (E92)

2010-2011

In 2010, BMW unveiled the ultimate M3 coupe, featuring the largest engine ever installed in a 3-Series by the German automaker.

The M3 GTS, famously featured in the "Need for Speed" games, was originally a track-focused coupe made-to-order by BMW. Celebrating the 25th anniversary of the E30 M3, it offered an option to be modified for road use. Based on the standard M3 body, it featured a new front bumper with a carbon-fiber splitter and functional grilles, while the hood had a power-dome and vents. Unique aerodynamic side-sills and a large adjustable rear wing enhanced its profile. Inside, it was stripped-down with lighter components, optional climate control, and no rear seats, but with a roll-cage for rigidity.

Under the hood, a tuned 4.4-liter V8 engine paired with a seven-speed dual-clutch gearbox delivered power to the rear wheels. The suspension was fully adjustable, complemented by a new brake system with vented and drilled discs for improved performance.

BMW M3 (F80)

2014-2018

After four generations of the M3 in different body styles, BMW's fifth-generation performance model was exclusively offered as a four-door sedan.

BMW decided to name its coupe versions with even numbers, so the 3-Series coupe became the 4-Series. However, the iconic M3 badge continued to grace the four-door BMW 3-Series.

In 2014, BMW introduced the M3, the sportiest variant of the 3-Series. Externally, it distinguished itself with a new front bumper featuring a larger spoiler and wider side air-intakes. Air-vents on the front fenders improved aerodynamics and reduced turbulence around the wheels. Unlike its predecessor, which had a hood bulge for a V8 engine, the M3's bulge was smaller for design and pedestrian safety. Unique 18-inch light-alloy wheels were fitted, and at the rear, a quad-exhaust system was integrated into a bespoke M3 diffuser.

Inside, the M3 showcased its motorsport heritage with high-bolstered seats, a special steering wheel, an exclusive instrument cluster, and M badge accents throughout the cabin.

The 2014 M3, known as the F80 model, marked a significant shift as the first turbocharged BMW M3. Its 3.0-liter inline-six engine offered more power and torque than the previous 4.0-liter V8 in the E92 M3. It came standard with a 6-speed manual transmission, with an optional 7-speed dual-clutch automatic gearbox available.

BMW M3 CS (F80)

2017-2018

Nearly three decades after the E30 M3 Evolution, BMW released a special version of the F80 M3: the M3 CS.

BMW has a history of making light, agile M3s, starting with the 1988 M3 Evolution. In 2017, they introduced the M3 CS, a lighter and more powerful version with better weight distribution and a higher speed limiter of 174 mph (280 kph).

The M3 CS featured a carbon-fiber-reinforced plastic (CFRP) hood and roof, reducing weight by 10 kg (22 lbs). It also had a unique carbon-fiber rear spoiler, a three-section front air intake, 19" front wheels, and 22" rear rims. The rear bumper had a diffuser and four exhaust pipes.

Inside, the M3 CS combined sportiness with luxury, offering two-tone leather upholstery with Alcantara accents, an advanced infotainment unit, and a Harman Kardon sound system.

With a lowered, stiffened suspension and a 460 hp engine, the M3 CS was faster than the regular M3. It sent power to the rear wheels via a seven-speed dual-clutch automatic transmission, making it 0.4 seconds quicker than the standard model.

BMW M3 Sedan

2020-2024

The BMW M3 is one of the brand's most iconic models, starting the "M-madness" in the mid-'80s and continuing ever since.

After BMW split its 3-Series into the 4-Series for coupes and the 3-Series for sedans and station wagons, rumors of the M3's retirement spread. However, the M3 continued to improve.

The seventh generation of the BMW 3 Series debuted in 2018, but M3 fans waited until 2020 for the top-spec version. The 2020 M3 featured a redesigned, more aggressive front bumper, a muscular stance, flared arches, and standard 18” light-alloy wheels. The rear had four exhausts, with an optional performance package that grouped the pipes in the middle.

Inside, it came standard with sport-bucket seats, a TFT color display instrument cluster, and an infotainment system with Apple CarPlay and Android support. Models with automatic gearboxes included paddle shifters.

Notably, the 2020 M3 introduced an all-wheel-drive system for the first time. The standard six-speed manual sent power to the rear wheels, while the xDrive version, available only with the 8-speed automatic, powered all four wheels.

BMW M3 CS

2023-Present

In 2023, BMW introduced an extreme version of its M3 sedan, continuing its tradition of lighter, higher-performance variants of this iconic model.

The history of BMW's lightweight models spans decades, from the 1972 CSL to the E46 M3 CSL and E92 CRT, all focused on performance through lighter construction. The 2024 M3 CS took this further by adding more power to the S58 TwinPower Turbo inline-six engine.

This limited edition featured a special "Green Goblin" color and black accents on the hood, front bumper, side sills, and roof, indicating carbon-fiber elements. The trunk lid had a carbon-fiber lip spoiler.

Inside, more carbon-fiber trims were added to the center console, dashboard, and steering wheel. BMW replaced the regular seats with carbon-fiber high-back sports seats in fabric and leather. The dual-screen setup was retained for essential driving information and infotainment use.

The engine received an additional 40 horsepower, and while the all-wheel-drive setup was standard, removing the speed limiter allowed speeds up to 302 kph (188 mph).

BMW M3 Touring

2022-2024

BMW introduced the first station wagon version of its top-performing M3 at the 2022 Goodwood Festival of Speed, catering to drivers seeking speed despite having growing families.

BMW first attempted to sell an M-badged station wagon with the M5 Touring in 1992, which saw limited success.

The M3 Touring, introduced in 2022, shared the front fascia with its sedan counterpart, including the tall kidney grille. The front bumper featured an air intake and side scoops for cooling. The profile revealed a long roof ending with a small spoiler, and the rear had a quad-exhaust system and diffuser similar to the sedan, with a lower bumper cut for the tailgate.

Inside, the M3 Touring featured a curved display and front seats from the M3 sedan. The back had a split-folding bench for up to three passengers, expanding the trunk from 500 to 1,510 liters, though the transmission tunnel made the middle seat less comfortable.

Under the hood, it had the same 503 PS (510 hp) inline-six TwinPower Turbo engine, paired with an eight-speed automatic gearbox, sending power to all four wheels.

BMW M3 Sedan

2024-Present

BMW refreshed the M3 lineup for the 2025 model year in the summer of 2024, with production starting in July for both Europe and North America.

The M3 badge was introduced in 1986 with the E30 M3, a highly successful racing car. Initially offered only as a two-door coupe or convertible, later generations saw changes in engine and body style. The E46 introduced a straight-six engine and was available as a coupe, convertible, and sedan. Most M3s featured six-cylinder engines, except for the V8-powered M3 GTR. BMW later returned to the inline-six, adding turbocharging technology.

In 2013, BMW split the 3 Series into the 4 Series (coupe, convertible, Gran Coupe) and the 3 Series (sedan, station wagon). Consequently, the M3 became available only as a four-door or station wagon. The seventh-generation M3 debuted in 2020, and a refreshed model was introduced in 2024, available as the M3 and M3 Competition.

The 2025 M3 featured new LED headlights with adaptive and Matrix-LED technology, a vertical kidney grille, and a sculptured hood with power domes. The lower bumper had angular air intakes and side scoops to cool the brakes. The profile showed aggressive vents, black side sills, and flared rear wheel arches. At the rear, it had a lip spoiler, split LED taillights, and a diffuser with four exhausts.

Inside, the M3 boasted high-bolstered front seats, carbon fiber options, and a curved dashboard panel with two screens for the driver and the new 8.5 BMW Operating System. The center console included controls for the automatic or six-speed manual transmission and adaptive dampers. The rear seats had space for two occupants, divided by a transmission tunnel.

Under the hood, the M3 retained the turbocharged straight-six engine, producing 473 hp for the manual rear-wheel drive and more power with the Competition package, which included all-wheel drive and an eight-speed automatic transmission.

BMW launched the M3 Competition Touring in summer 2024, combining family practicality with sports car performance. M4

The BMW M4 is a fast and sporty coupe made for high performance, known for its powerful engine and sleek design. It's loved by drivers who enjoy both driving on the road and on the track.

BMW M4 Convertible

2014-2020

BMW unveiled the M-version of the 4 Series at the 2014 Los Angeles Auto Show, continuing the legacy of M-powered convertibles that began with the E30 M3 in the 1980s.

When BMW introduced the 4 Series in 2014, replacing the 3 Series coupe and convertible, fans had to adapt. The M3 coupe was replaced by the M4 in coupe and convertible forms. Despite weighing 250 kilograms (551 lbs) more than its coupe counterpart, the M4 Convertible retained a similar design and offered a retractable hard top. Up front, it featured the distinctive pinched headlights flanking the kidney grille and a wide center air intake on the bumper. Side scoops cooled the front rotors, while the profile included extracting vents on the fenders and specific side sills. At the rear, it sported a four-exhaust system and a diffuser.

Inside, the M4 Convertible had high-bolstered seats for stability during cornering, though rear legroom was limited due to the retractable roof mechanism. The driver faced an analog instrument cluster with a digital display, and atop the center stack was the infotainment screen controlled via a rotary knob. Carbon fiber trims and leather upholstery were standard.

Under the hood, the M4 Convertible housed a turbocharged inline-six engine producing 431 PS (425 HP), sent to the rear wheels through either a six-speed manual or a seven-speed M Double Clutch gearbox, equipped with an electronically controlled limited-slip differential.

BMW M4 Coupe (F82)

2014-2020

Back in 1985, BMW introduced the M3, a successful sports car in touring motorsport. Fast forward to the M4, it's essentially the coupe version of the M3.

BMW M5 (E28)

1985-1988

The 1985 E28 was the first BMW 5 Series to wear the M5 badge, blending sports car performance with luxurious sedan comfort.

The BMW M5 debuted in late 1985 as a successor to the M535i, arriving almost four years after its predecessor. Unlike its predecessor, the M5 offered more than just increased power; it featured unique exterior styling and a luxurious interior.

At the front, it retained the E28's four-headlight design but sported a black grille with an M5 badge and BMW Motorsport stripes. The lower bumper included rectangular fog lamps and turn signals, complemented by an added apron for a sportier appearance.

Its profile hinted at performance with a forward-leaning stance, BBS multi-spoke alloy wheels, and black window trims, departing from the chrome-heavy styling of its era. At the rear, options included a flat spoiler and center-mounted dual exhausts reminiscent of the M535i.

Inside, the M5 boasted a sporty cabin with cloth or leather upholstery. The front seats featured high bolsters and BMW Motorsport's signature colors embroidered on the seatbacks. The driver faced a specialized instrument cluster with a prominent tachometer and onboard computer panel. The center console housed the gear stick and power window controls, with a rear bench seat for two passengers.

Under the hood, the M5 housed the M88/3 inline-six engine, delivering 282 horsepower from its 3.5-liter displacement. Power flowed to the rear wheels via a five-speed manual gearbox and a limited-slip differential. Enhanced by BMW Motorsport with a stiffer suspension and gas-filled struts, the E28 M5 set a new benchmark, sparking a horsepower rivalry among German automakers.

The E34 M5, launched in 1988, featured a slightly larger engine with significantly increased power. BMW Motorsport also upgraded the suspension, brakes, and transmission. While BMW aimed for a refined exterior—not as bold as the flamboyant M3—the E34 M5 still had subtle enhancements.

Initially powered by a 3.5-liter naturally aspirated engine producing 315 PS (311 hp), it was the sole version available in the U.S., with 8,344 units produced.
