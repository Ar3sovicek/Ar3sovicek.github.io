BMW X1

The BMW X1 is a compact luxury SUV that blends practicality, performance, and style. Launched in 2009, it offers a versatile interior, efficient turbocharged engines, and a smooth driving experience. The X1 includes advanced technology like a user-friendly infotainment system and driver assistance features. Its sporty design and premium feel make it a popular choice in the luxury compact SUV market.

BMW X1 (E84)

2009-2012

The newest and smallest member of the lineup, the X1, was the missing piece in BMW's X range.

The X1's exterior design is reminiscent of its bigger brother, the X3, with taillights and headlights inspired by the 7-Series from 2008. Built on the 3 Series platform, it offers ample cabin space and great cargo capacity.

The interior features high-quality materials and advanced technology, providing good headroom and legroom for all passengers. The luggage compartment can expand from 420 L to 1,350 L with the rear seats folded.

For safety, the X1 includes Dynamic Stability Control, optional Performance Control, and frontal and side airbags. Optional features include xenon headlights, adaptive headlights, and a high-beam assistant.

The X1 also comes with the latest BMW iDrive system and optional Business and Professional navigation. It offers precise handling and good steering feedback, though the steering can be heavy at lower speeds.

Starting at $43,000, the X1 offers many optional features, though they can be pricey.

BMW X1 (E84)

2012-2015

For the 2014 model year, BMW refreshed the X1 lineup, improving both its appearance and drivetrain.

The X1, based on the E90 3 Series platform, was introduced in 2009 as a premium small-sized crossover. By 2012, after selling over 275,000 units, BMW unveiled a facelifted version at the New York International Auto Show, boosting sales to over 150,000 units for 2013 and 2014. The E84 model was highly successful.

The facelift included a revised front fascia with new headlights, grille, and a redesigned lower bumper with a larger painted area. LED daytime running lights were added to the Xenon light package, and turn signals were integrated into the door mirrors.

Inside, changes included a new dashboard and center console for automatic transmission models, with new knobs, switches, and a joystick-like gear selector. However, the back seat still had limited room due to the tall transmission tunnel.

Under the hood, BMW introduced a new inline-four engine, available with or without a turbocharger.

BMW X1

2016-2019

In 2016, BMW introduced the second generation of its smallest SUV, the X1, which was no longer based on the 3 Series platform.

The first generation of the X1 was popular, but customers complained about the cramped interior. To address this, BMW switched to the UKL2 platform, shared with MINI, for the second generation introduced in 2016. This platform allowed for a transversely-mounted engine, resulting in a shorter nose and overhang, and a roomier interior with wider front and rear doors.

The front design featured an enlarged kidney grille and headlights similar to the X3. Inside, the new X1 offered more space, with bolstered front seats and a tall center console housing the gear selector. The dashboard included two large dials and an LCD for onboard information, while the infotainment screen was controlled by a rotary dial. The new architecture provided better legroom and trunk space.

The second-generation X1 came with either front or all-wheel drive, and transmission options included a six-speed manual, an eight-speed automatic, a six-speed automatic, or a seven-speed dual-clutch gearbox, depending on the version.

BMW X1

2019-2022

The BMW X1 is crucial for BMW, being its top-selling model globally in 2018. After four years, it received a facelift. The updated X1 features a bold front bumper with larger air intakes. The front end is completely redesigned, highlighted by a larger BMW kidney grille and new LED headlights.

Inside the cabin, the X1 combines style with advanced technology. It offers three trim versions: Sport Line, xLine, and M Sport, each enhancing the base equipment. Comfort is a priority for all seats, with the rear bench featuring a 60:40 split and individual sliding seat surfaces that can move up to 13 cm (5.1 in).

Under the hood, BMW provides a range of engines including three and four-cylinder options, both turbocharged gasoline and turbodiesel. There's also a Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle (PHEV) variant. The entry-level model features a 1.5-liter three-cylinder turbodiesel paired with a 6-speed manual or 7-speed dual-clutch gearbox, available in front-wheel-drive (sDrive). At the top end, the X1 xDrive25i boasts a 2.0-liter turbocharged gasoline engine delivering 231 hp, achieving 0-100 kph (62 mph) in 6.5 seconds.

For efficiency, the xDrive25e Plug-in Hybrid combines a 1.5-liter turbocharged gasoline engine with a 70kW electric motor, producing a total of 224 hp.

BMW X3 (E83)

2004-2007

After the BMW X5 found success, BMW quickly followed up with a smaller version, launching the first generation of the BMW X3 in 2003.

In fall 2003, at the Frankfurt Auto Show, BMW introduced the smaller sibling of the X5. The X3 had few competitors at the time, notably the Land Rover Freelander, also developed under BMW's guidance. Built on the BMW 3-Series platform, it shared rear suspension with the compact sedan.

Its design, resembling a station wagon on elevated stilts, was a contentious point, especially its distinctive headlights, which were revised in a 2007 facelift.

Inside, the X3 comfortably seated five adults with ample headroom and decent rear legroom. The dashboard offered optional iDrive infotainment, controlled via a central rotary knob. Entertainment options included a BMW AM/FM/M-CD Radio with eight speakers, and a panoramic sunroof was available as an option.

The X3 featured BMW's all-wheel-drive system, developed with Magna Steyr in Austria where the SUV was manufactured. Standard across models, the system provided a default 40:60 torque split between front and rear axles, capable of directing up to 100 percent of engine torque to either axle via an electronically controlled multiple-plate clutch.

Engine choices included 2.0-liter and 3.0-liter options, with four and six cylinders respectively, delivering varying levels of power and performance.
