Iconic and balanced

First generation

The first generation of the BMW 3 Series, starting in 1975, set the stage for what would become an iconic car. It prioritized driving enjoyment with rear-wheel drive and precise handling. Its design was classic and sporty, inside and out. With a variety of engine options, it offered both power and efficiency. This generation established the 3 Series as a leader in the compact executive car segment.

BMW 3 Series Coupe (E21)

1975-1983

The debut of the initial 3-Series in 1975 followed the triumph of the BMW 02 Series, marking another milestone in the company's legacy. It swiftly became BMW's most prosperous series.

In the early '70s, BMW stood out in the competitive motorsport scene by focusing on lightweight, daily-driver-friendly vehicles that also performed well on the track. The first generation of the 3-Series, sporting a distinctive "shark-nose" design, offered a range of engine options, some featuring twin headlights. Inside, it accommodated four passengers with a dashboard angled toward the driver. Transmission options included a standard 4 or 5-speed manual and, for certain engines, a 3-speed automatic. The top-tier 323i boasted a Bosch mechanical fuel injection system and various manual gearbox choices, including one with a dog-leg pattern.

Second generation

The second generation of the BMW 3 Series, starting in 1982, brought some upgrades while keeping the sporty feel. It had a sleeker design and more comfortable interior. Engine options included both four-cylinder and six-cylinder choices, with improved fuel injection technology for better performance and efficiency. BMW also introduced new models like the Convertible and Touring wagon. Overall, it continued the 3 Series' reputation for a great driving experience with added comfort and versatility.

BMW 3 Series Sedan (E30)

1982-1992

In 1982, BMW debuted a new generation of the 3 Series, marking a significant advancement from its predecessor. Notably, this generation introduced the highly sought-after four-door variant, expanding the model's appeal and accessibility.

The first-generation 3 Series was a hit, captivating nearly 1.4 million drivers worldwide, but it lacked a four-door option. This gap was filled with the arrival of the E30, the second-generation 3 Series.

Known for its quad-headlights and distinctive black grille with horizontal slats, the E30 boasted a sleek design. The sedan shared the same wheelbase as its coupe counterpart. Inside, the driver-oriented dashboard featured HVAC controls below the stereo, though not all models had AC. Bucket seats were standard up front, with a center console separating the driver's area. In the back, a bench provided seating for three.

Under the hood, BMW offered a range of engines paired with manual or automatic transmissions, all powering the rear wheels.

BMW 3 Series Coupe (E30)

1982-1992

In 1982, BMW unveiled the E30, the second generation of its compact 3-Series. This model marked a significant milestone for the German automaker.

The E30's design was understated yet captivating, with clean lines and rounded headlights. It came in both two-door and four-door versions, offering engines to suit various needs and budgets. In 1987, a refresh updated the exterior with black detailing and redesigned lights. Engine options ranged from a 1.8-liter to a 2.7-liter, with the 320 iS delivering an impressive 192 hp from a 2.0-liter engine. The E30 also introduced innovations like turbocharged diesel engines and optional all-wheel drive.

BMW 3 Series Cabriolet (E30)

1986-1993

In 1986, four years into the second generation of the 3-Series, BMW introduced a convertible version of the popular model.

BMW's 3-Series Cabriolet, launched in 1986, marked a departure from its predecessor by offering a fully convertible design. It retained the iconic double headlights and black grille at the front, showcasing a timeless minimalist design. Inside, it mirrored the coupe's layout, featuring a driver-oriented dashboard and angular instrument cluster. With a choice of engines ranging from a four-cylinder 1.8-liter to a 2.5-liter inline-six, paired with manual or automatic transmissions, it offered a balance of performance and comfort.

BMW 3 Series Touring (E30)

1988-1993

The second generation of the 3-Series debuted in 1982, but it wasn't until the mid-life refresh that the station wagon version was introduced, marking the first appearance of the Touring name in the 3-Series lineup.

In the mid-'80s, the station wagon trend was gaining momentum, but premium brands like BMW hadn't yet entered that market segment. While Mercedes-Benz and Audi had offerings, BMW didn't have a compact station wagon in its lineup.

The 3-Series Touring changed that, sharing most of its design with the sedan up to the B-pillars. Beyond that, it featured a longer roof, distinct rear quarter panels, and D-pillars. The tailgate was innovative, with a window that could open independently.

Inside, it offered similar headroom to the sedan, with rear seats that folded down to extend trunk space. Engine options included 4 and 6 cylinders, both gasoline and diesel, with the addition of all-wheel-drive for the 325 iX version.

Third generation

The third generation of the BMW 3 Series, starting in 1990, brought a sleeker design and better performance. Inside, it was more luxurious. It offered powerful engines and introduced new safety features like traction and stability control. BMW also added new models like the Compact for city drivers. Overall, it continued the 3 Series' tradition of blending sportiness with comfort and technology.

BMW 3 Series Sedan (E36)

1991-1998

The BMW 3-Series E30 was a legendary success in the compact premium-car market, but by 1991, it was time for a successor.

The second generation of the BMW 3-Series, launched in the early '90s, embraced a more modern design language compared to its predecessor. It resembled a scaled-down version of the 5-Series, with wider dimensions and a sleeker profile. The front end featured widened "BMW kidney" grilles and sleek headlights, contributing to its improved aerodynamics. Inside, there was more legroom and standard power steering and brakes across the range. Optional amenities included power windows, air-conditioning, airbags, and a premium audio system. Engine options ranged from a 1.6-liter to a 3.2-liter for the M3, with turbocharged diesel variants also available.

BMW 3 Series Coupe (E36)

1992-1998

The BMW 3 Series began as a coupe in the '70s but expanded into various body styles over the years. However, the coupe remained significant, especially in the third generation, which debuted in late 1990 and hit the market in 1991.

The E36 brought significant changes to the 3-series design, including new twin headlamps and frameless windows in the coupe. While some fans were initially unsure about the changes, the car offered improvements in suspension, wheelbase, and engine size, making it a top choice in its class.

Inside, the dashboard was driver-focused with accessible controls and a comfortable layout. The seating position was low, appealing to those seeking a sporty feel. In the rear, there was ample space for two adults, and various trim levels offered options like bolstered seats, heating, and power adjustments.

Under the hood, the E36 initially kept some engines from its predecessor but later received updates, including a newly developed 2.8-liter engine in the 328i model. The standard transmission was a 5-speed manual, with a 4-speed automatic available as an option.

BMW 3 Series Cabriolet (E36)

1993-1999

BMW launched the third generation of the 3 Series in 1990, offering it in various body styles. However, the convertible version wasn't introduced until 1993.

BMW initially didn't anticipate the demand for a premium convertible, which delayed its introduction until 1993. However, once launched, the E36 convertible exceeded expectations. It shared design elements with the coupe version, featuring squared headlights instead of the quad-round design of its predecessor. The lack of a B-pillar gave it a sporty roadster-like appearance. Inside, there was more room for passengers compared to the previous model, though rear legroom was still limited. The convertible offered both manual and sportier automatic transmission options, with engine choices ranging from a sluggish 1.8-liter to more powerful options.

BMW 3 Series Compact (E36/5)

1994-2000

BMW revisited its past with the idea of bringing back the 2002 Touring model from 1971, a hatchback based on the 2002 platform. This concept was considered for revival in the form of the 1994 E36 Compact.

BMW responded to a growing interest in upscale compact hatchbacks by launching the 3 Series Compact in 1994. It resembled the E36 3 Series from the front but featured a tailgate at the back. Despite being cheaper to produce, it didn't outsell its siblings. Its rear-wheel-drive setup, competing against front-wheel-drive models like the Golf, didn't resonate with buyers as expected. The rear axle, inherited from the previous E30 generation, also posed challenges.

To streamline production, many body panels were shared with the E36 sedan, resulting in rectangular headlights and a kidney grille integrated into the bumper. The profile resembled the sedan, with sporty touches and frameless doors. Inside, it mirrored the E36 coupe but with lower-quality materials. The rear offered minimal space for passengers but had a decent trunk. Engine options included gasoline and diesel variants with four or six cylinders, paired with a manual or automatic transmission.

Fourth generation

The fourth generation of the BMW 3 Series, starting in 1998, brought a more modern look, better engines, and new tech features. It had sleeker styling outside and more luxurious materials inside. The engines were more powerful and fuel-efficient, and new technology like variable valve timing and all-wheel drive improved performance. The fourth generation set new standards for luxury and innovation in its class.

BMW 3 Series (E46)

1998-2002

In 1998, BMW launched the fourth generation of the 3-Series. While not a dramatic visual departure, it represented a significant technological advancement.

The E46 generation of the 3-Series began its design process in 1993 and hit the market in April 1998. Its design showcased clear headlights and a distinctive grille, while inside, it boasted a well-arranged instrument cluster. Under the hood, it introduced engines with over 100 hp even in base models, along with the return of the all-wheel-drive system and a new 2.0-liter turbodiesel engine. Transmission options included 5- or 6-speed manuals and 4- or 5-speed automatics.

BMW 3 Series Coupe (E46)

1999-2003

In 1999, BMW unveiled a new 3 Series Coupe, built on their advanced E46 platform.

The 1999 BMW 3 Series Coupe brought significant updates to its exterior design, featuring a longer chassis, revamped front grille, larger air intakes, and redesigned headlights. It boasted a stiffer suspension than its sedan counterpart, aiming to rival the Mercedes-Benz CLK and Volvo C70 in the premium coupe segment. New features included Automatic Stability Control with Traction (ASC+T) and Cornering Brake Control (CBC). In 2003, it received minor restyling with updated wings, front bumper, and lights. Compared to the E36, the E46 was longer, wider, and lower, enhancing stability and power. Safety features included dual front, side, and curtain airbags, along with ABS, ESC, traction control, cornering brake control, and electronic brake force distribution. Initially offered with five gasoline engines ranging from 2.2 to 3.0 liters, with power outputs between 125 kW and 170 kW, paired with either a 5-speed manual or automatic transmission.

BMW 3 Series Touring

1999-2001

BMW rolled out the fourth generation of the 3 Series starting with the sedan in 1997, followed by the coupe in 1998, and the station wagon, known as the Touring, in 1999.

The fourth generation of the 3 Series, known as the E46, brought a more family-friendly approach without losing its sporty edge. It was a hit in Europe but didn't catch on as much in the U.S., partly due to fewer engine options. Designed to be less aggressive than its predecessor, it maintained the iconic BMW look. The sedan and Touring (wagon) shared panels up to the B-pillar, with distinct designs afterward.

Inside, it offered comfortable seating and ample space. The Touring boasted a practical rear split-folding seatback and options like a window-only tailgate for easy loading. Engine choices ranged from 1.9 to 3.0 liters, with manual or automatic transmissions, and rear- or all-wheel drive options.

BMW 3 Series Cabriolet

2000-2003

Following the sedan's launch in 1998, BMW expanded the fourth-generation 3 Series lineup. The coupe and station wagon debuted in 1999, with the convertible following in 2000 to meet customer demand.

The E46 Cabriolet, BMW's third-generation 3 Series convertible, was an improvement over its predecessor. It featured a sleek design borrowed from the coupe, offering more space in the rear for passengers. With a variety of engine options, it catered to both economical and sporty preferences. The front design mirrored the coupe, showcasing distinctive headlights and kidney grilles. Inside, bolstered seats provided comfort, although rear legroom was somewhat compromised due to the convertible mechanism. Engine choices were plentiful, with both manual and automatic transmission options available.

BMW 3 Series Touring (E46)

2001-2005

In 2001, BMW revamped the 3-Series to better compete with the Mercedes C-Class, enhancing its features and design.

When the E46 3-Series Touring hit the scene in 1998, BMW was already seasoned in offering a sporty station wagon for families. By 2001, it was time for a refresh. The exterior saw minor tweaks, with updated headlights and bumper. Inside, there were new steering wheel options and paddle shifters for the automated transmission. The center console got more buttons for safety features, and the infotainment system upgraded to a color screen for navigation. Under the hood, new engines were introduced, including a 2.0-liter unit with 143 hp, a first for BMW since the first generation. The top model boasted a powerful 3.0-liter engine with 231 hp, available with rear or all-wheel drive and a 6-speed manual. Automatic transmission was an option for all versions.

BMW 3 Series Compact (E46)

2001-2005

BMW aimed to broaden its customer base by introducing the 3-Series Compact in 1993, with a second generation following in 2000. However, the model didn't perform as expected.

BMW upgraded the 3-Series Compact in its second generation, moving it onto the E46 platform for better performance. The goal was to attract younger customers with a more affordable BMW option. Similarly, Mercedes-Benz attempted a similar strategy with the C-Class Sport Coupe in 2001, but both efforts fell short. The Compact featured a distinct front fascia with unique headlights and a shorter body with a tailgate instead of a separate trunk lid. Inside, it shared similarities with the E46 coupe, offering similar interior space. Engine options ranged from 1.6-liter to 2.5-liter gasoline and diesel engines, but the car's design and lightweight rear end led to frequent oversteering issues, undermining its appeal.

BMW 3 Series (E46)

2002-2005

The fourth generation of the BMW 3-Series debuted in 1998 and quickly became a top seller in the premium compact segment. However, by 2000, the Mercedes-Benz C-Class had surpassed its sales figures.

BMW anticipated the need for a facelift for the 3-Series soon after its launch in 1998. The refreshed version, introduced in 2002, brought subtle exterior changes like redesigned headlights and taillights, along with minor interior updates such as a new steering wheel design with paddle-shifters for certain models. However, the most significant changes were in the engine lineup, with options ranging from a 1.8-liter base model to a top-of-the-line 3.0-liter engine offering 231 horsepower. Transmission options included both manual and automatic, available with rear or all-wheel-drive systems.

BMW 3 Series Coupe (E46)

2003-2006

Five years after launching the BMW E46 3 Series Coupe, BMW unveiled the updated version called the LCI, or Life Cycle Impulse.

BMW's design team, led by Chris Bangle, faced a challenge with the E46 Coupe. They aimed to refresh its timeless design without losing its appeal. The update brought subtle changes to the front and rear, along with new bumper designs and alloy wheel options. Inside, enhancements included the addition of XM Sirius Satellite radio and navigation, prompting a redesigned center stack. Under the hood, engine upgrades were significant to maintain competitiveness, proving that larger engines could be fuel-efficient. The Coupe maintained its classic profile, with reshaped headlights and LED taillights. Inside, frameless doors added to the premium feel, while the cabin offered bolstered seats and optional leather upholstery. The instrument cluster received a refresh, and customers had audio and navigation system choices. Power came from a range of diesel and gasoline engines, with rear-wheel drive standard.

BMW 3 Series Cabriolet (E46)

2003-2007

After five years, BMW refreshed the fourth-generation 3 Series, the E46, with an overdue facelift.

BMW switched from calling updated models "facelifted" to using the term LCI (Life Cycle Impulse). In 2003, with the 3 Series as their top-seller, BMW faced tough competition in the convertible segment. The E46 Convertible LCI got aesthetic updates, new engines, and improved cabins. It introduced features previously unseen in a 3 Series.

Exterior changes included revamped headlights with a single curved line, a redesigned front bumper with broader air intakes, and fog lamp-equipped scoops. The profile maintained its sleek lines with slightly flared wheel arches. The low-mounted engine improved visibility and safety, while the power retractable roof could be operated remotely.

Inside, a redesigned dashboard and expanded options were introduced. The instrument panel featured classic BMW dials with a new LCD display below. The center stack offered a new option for a sat-nav system, and the center console housed controls for power windows and transmission. Rear legroom was minimal, but customers could opt for a wind-stopper net.

Under the hood, new gasoline and turbodiesel engines were available, paired with manual or automatic transmissions. All variants were rear-wheel drive.

Fifth generation

The fifth generation of the BMW 3 Series, starting in 2005, brought a bolder design and better technology. Inside, it was more luxurious. The engines were more powerful and efficient, thanks to new technology like turbocharging and direct injection. BMW also introduced features like iDrive and advanced driver assistance systems. Overall, it continued the 3 Series' tradition of setting new standards for driving enjoyment and luxury.

BMW 3 Series Touring (E91)

2005-2008

After the huge success of the 4th generation 3-Series, known as the E46, BMW decided it was time to introduce a brand new model that would continue to impress.

The new 3-Series, known as the E90 (sedan), E91 (touring), E92 (coupe), and E93 (cabriolet), featured a completely redesigned body inspired by the 5-Series. With a larger wheelbase compared to the E46, passenger comfort improved, and boot space increased due to the use of run-flat tires. The E90 boasted a 25% stiffer body for enhanced safety, complemented by features like ABS, dynamic stability control, and traction control. Its timeless design appealed to buyers, maintaining its value over time. With a range of engines offering both power and fuel efficiency, the E90 delivered a great driving experience, characterized by precise steering and excellent handling, catering to discerning drivers.

BMW 3 Series (E90)

2005-2008

In 2005, BMW unveiled the fifth generation of the 3 Series at the Geneva Motor Show, showcasing a bold design influenced by Chris Bangle's vision.

BMW faced a tough challenge when they tasked American designer Chris Bangle with crafting a fresh design language for their traditionally styled brand. By 2005, his "Flame Surfacing" concept was already known, but previous models like the 7 Series, 5 Series, and Z4 had polarized opinions. The E90 3 Series, overseen by Japanese stylist Joji Nagashima, presented a new challenge.

The E90's front showcased angular headlights and a narrow kidney grille integrated into the bumper, a departure from the previous model's fluid shapes. The sedan's longer wheelbase and increased size aligned it with European compact standards. Inside, BMW used higher-quality materials, offering bolstered or sports seats and two dashboard designs. The instrument cluster featured a classic layout, with an LCD displaying car data below.

Under the hood, the E90 offered a range of engines, from four to six cylinders, with both gasoline and turbodiesel options. Transmission choices included six-speed manuals or automatics, with the availability of xDrive all-wheel drive systems for most inline-six versions.

BMW 3 Series Coupe (E92)

2006-2010

The 3 Series Coupe hit the market in 2006, following the sedan by a year. While this timing wasn't unusual, the coupe's strikingly different styling from its sedan counterpart surprised many loyal BMW enthusiasts.

The first 3 Series debuted in 1975 as a two-door model. The sedan version arrived in 1983, and by 2005, the fifth generation was launched first as a sedan and later as a coupe in 2006. While the coupe retained the sedan's strong performance, it had its own distinctive look, with unique body panels and aggressive headlights. Inside, it shared its dashboard with the sedan but had limited rear seating for two passengers due to a high transmission tunnel. The coupe offered engines starting from 2 liters and up, omitting smaller options available in the sedan.

BMW 3 Series Cabriolet (E93)

2007-2010

The BMW E93 is the convertible version of the highly popular 3 Series. It marks the final iteration of the 3 Series convertible, as subsequent generations are sold under the 4 Series name.

The BMW E93, the convertible version of the 3 Series, introduces several significant innovations. Firstly, it boasts the brand's first turbocharged gasoline engine, particularly powerful in the 335i variant, which outputs 306 horsepower from its 3.0-liter straight-six engine, paired with a twin-turbo.

This model also features BMW's first retractable hardtop, providing enhanced safety and a sleek appearance. The car looks equally impressive with the top up or down, and the "Comfort Access" feature allows remote operation of the roof using the car's key.

Inside, the E93 offers a range of premium trim options, including Dark Burl Walnut wood, Bamboo Anthracite wood, Light Burl Walnut wood, and Aluminum trims. Additionally, BMW incorporates innovative sun-reflective technology into the leather upholstery, significantly reducing surface temperatures by up to 36 degrees Fahrenheit for dark colors and 27 degrees Fahrenheit for light colors.

BMW 3 Series Touring (E91)

2008-2012

In 2008, BMW refreshed the entire 3-Series lineup, including the station wagon variant called Sports Wagon in North America and Touring in Europe.

To meet Euro 5a emission standards, BMW revamped the 3-Series lineup in 2008, making significant changes to the engines and other aspects. Notably, the front grille now had rounded edges, and the lower bumper featured a new design. LED taillights were introduced, and the interior saw updates, including adopting the joystick-style automatic transmission selector from the 5-Series. The rear bench could be split-folded, expanding trunk space. Engine options included a 2.0-liter gasoline unit with 136 hp and a fuel-efficient 115 hp turbo-diesel, along with the introduction of the xDrive designation for all-wheel-drive models.

BMW 3 Series (E90)

2008-2011

In 2008, BMW upgraded the 3-Series to meet the Euro 5 norms effective from January 2009.

After four successful years on the market, BMW upgraded the 3-Series engine lineup and made additional improvements.

The main exterior change was the redesigned "kidney" grille with rounded interior edges and a chromed top rim integrated into the front fascia. The lower bumper featured a "smiling" cut, and the rear received new LED taillights with smaller reversing lights.

Inside, the most notable update was the automatic transmission selector, which adopted the joy-stick system from the 5-Series. The optional iDrive system now included voice controls and shortcut buttons.

Under the hood, BMW introduced a 2.0-liter naturally aspirated gasoline engine with 136 hp, and a fuel-efficient 115 hp turbo-diesel engine paired with a 6-speed manual. The all-wheel-drive versions were renamed xDrive.

BMW 3 Series Cabriolet (E93)

2010-2013

After updating the 3 Series Sedan and Touring (E90 and E91), BMW refreshed the coupe and convertible versions of its popular lineup.

In 2009, BMW introduced new Euro 5-compliant vehicles and made additional updates. The open-top version received significant improvements.

At the front, new headlights featured an angrier design and updated daytime running lights. The front fascia got a new bumper with a wide lower grille. The rear sported new taillights with smaller reversing lights.

Inside, the automatic transmission now used a joystick-style gear selector, and the paddle shifters behind the steering wheel were smaller.

The biggest change under the hood was the introduction of the 335i, which featured a 3.0-liter turbocharged engine.

BMW 3 Series Coupe (E92)

2010-2013

BMW introduced the LCI version of the 3 Series coupe, the E92, with significant updates beyond just new wheels and minor styling changes.

The first generation of the 3 Series, launched in the early '70s, was available only as a coupe. Starting with the second generation, BMW introduced a four-door version, along with convertible and station wagon options. However, the 3 Series coupe remained particularly popular among fans. The E92 coupe debuted in 2006 and received an update after four years, like the rest of the 3 Series models.

The facelifted version featured a new, broader grille and stylish horizontal silver slats in the lower bumper grille. Standard bi-xenon headlights in the U.S. market now included white circles for daytime running lights. Higher-powered versions like the 335i had a distinctive power bulge on the hood. The updated LED taillights at the back featured smaller reversing lights.

Inside, the 3 Series coupe LCI (Life Cycle Impulse) emphasized comfort and premium materials, with base models featuring walnut trims and aluminum finishes available as options.

Under the hood, BMW offered a wide range of engines, from 141 PS to a new 320 PS (316 HP) powerplant. Depending on the engine choice and options, power could be sent to the rear or all four wheels.

Sixth generation

The sixth generation of the BMW 3 Series, starting in 2012, brought a sleeker design and more advanced technology. Inside, it was more luxurious. It offered powerful and efficient engines, thanks to innovations like turbocharging. BMW also introduced new features like electric power steering and start-stop technology for better fuel economy. Overall, it continued the 3 Series' tradition of blending performance, luxury, and innovation.

BMW 3 Series Touring (F31)

2012-2016

BMW introduced the sixth generation of the 3 Series sedan in late 2011, and the station wagon version followed in 2012.

Since the second generation of the 3 Series, BMW recognized the need for a station wagon in their premium compact lineup. They continually improved the vehicle, offering unique features like an opening panoramic glass roof to make it more appealing.

With the sixth generation, BMW focused on sportier styling. The F31 Touring (station wagon) featured narrowed inner headlights, a broad lower grille with fog lamps, a sloped-down roof, and a raked-forward tailgate for a dynamic look.

Inside, the front design matched the sedan, while the rear had a 40/20/40 split-folding bench, expanding the trunk space from 495 liters (17.5 cu-ft) to 1,500 liters (53 cu-ft). The Touring also offered better headroom for rear passengers, though the middle seat legroom was limited by the transmission tunnel.

Under the hood, BMW provided a range of engines from 134 hp (136 PS) to 302 hp (306 PS), with options for a six-speed manual or eight-speed automatic transmission, and rear- or all-wheel drive systems.

BMW 3 Series (F30)

2012-2016

The sixth generation of the 3 Series was unveiled in late 2011 as a 2012 model, continuing its tradition of offering a sporty driving experience and premium quality to attract buyers.

BMW’s best-selling model, the 3 Series, has been on the market since 1975, initially as a two-door coupe. By 2012, the coupe became the 4 Series, but the four-door sedan remained a top model, featuring a wider range of engines, including diesel, gasoline, and hybrid options.

The sixth generation, designated as F30, introduced advanced design and technology compared to the E90. It featured pinched headlights, a broad grille, and a redesigned bumper. An M-package offered larger air intakes. The design emphasized a long hood and short rear deck, with a crease from the front fenders to the taillights. Exhaust configurations varied by engine.

Inside, high-quality materials were standard, with options for sports bucket seats. The instrument cluster was clean, with an iDrive screen above the center vents. Rear legroom was limited due to the engine layout and transmission tunnel.

Under the hood, BMW offered turbocharged engines with three, four, and six cylinders, both gasoline and diesel. Most models had a six-speed manual, with an eight-speed automatic available. The 3 Series came with either rear or all-wheel drive.

BMW 3 Series Gran Turismo

2013-2016

Based on the 3 Series platform, the 3 GT aimed to combine the practicality of a station wagon with the look of a sedan, offering a five-door liftgate with a coupe-like shape and a practical cabin.

Creating a new shape for traditional BMW buyers was a bold move. The German automaker aimed to attract customers who liked coupe styling but needed four doors. Inspired by Audi’s success with the A5/A7 Sportback, BMW introduced the 3 GT, sharing its technical platform with the sixth generation 3 Series and offering unique options unavailable in the 3 Series sedan.

The front design was similar to the rest of the 3 Series, with swept-back headlights flanking the broad kidney grille. It featured two bumper options: a regular one with triangular side scoops and an M-version with taller scoops and a wide lower grille. The main difference was the profile, with a longer greenhouse and a sloped-down tailgate, plus a third set of side windows. The GT also had a retractable wing on the tailgate.

Inside, the GT had taller seats for better visibility and a dashboard similar to the regular 3 Series. The rear bench seat comfortably fit three adults, and an optional panoramic glass roof brought more light into the cabin. The large trunk could hold two large golf bags.

The 3 Series GT offered a range of engines from 184 PS (181 hp) to 313 PS (308 hp), paired with either a six-speed manual or an eight-speed automatic transmission. It was available with rear-wheel or all-wheel drive, depending on the engine and options.

BMW 3 Series Gran Turismo LCI (F34)

2016-Present

Three years after its debut at the 2013 Geneva Motor Show, the BMW 3-Series Gran Turismo received a facelift.

In a departure from the usual practice of updating all body styles simultaneously, BMW chose a staggered approach for refreshing its lineup. While the 3-Series received its facelift in 2015, the GT version followed suit in 2016. Despite this strategic maneuver, sales figures fell short of expectations.

The updated 3-Series GT boasted a revamped front end featuring new LED headlights, with optional adaptive LED headlights available for added functionality. The daytime driving lights encircling the lamps added a distinctive touch, while the M Sport Package offered a more aggressive aesthetic with unique front and rear aprons, as well as side sills.

Moving inside, the cabin saw upgrades in materials, with a focus on higher quality throughout. The Center Information Display adopted a sleeker design with a narrower profile grille, while high-gloss black trim adorned the controls for the radio and air conditioning, complemented by chrome applications for a refined look.

Under the hood, the 2016 3-Series GT introduced a new version for the 325d Gran Turismo and brought in new gasoline engines from the BMW EfficientDynamics family. Notably, the lineup included rear- or all-wheel-drive options for all gasoline versions, with select diesel models also featuring all-wheel-drive as standard. This update aimed to enhance performance and efficiency while offering greater flexibility to cater to diverse customer preferences.

BMW 3 Series Touring (F31) LCI

2016-2019

In 2016, BMW rolled out a facelift for the sixth-generation 3-Series, covering the entire lineup, including the station wagon. The update brought some minor exterior tweaks and included technological enhancements.

Apart from the first generation, all subsequent iterations of the 3 Series offered a station wagon variant. The F31 Touring, introduced in 2012 and updated in 2016, marked the fifth generation of this versatile model.

Externally, the LCI brought a revised apron with updated air intakes and integrated sensors for optional features like Active Cruise Control. Full-LED headlights became available, while the rear received a new apron and standard LED lights.

Inside, a revamped center console featured a sliding cover for cup holders, now also accommodating mobile phones. The instrument cluster retained its classic four-dial setup, complemented by a widescreen infotainment unit on the center stack. The split-folding rear seats increased trunk space to 1500 liters (53 cu-ft).

Under the hood, the 2016 3 Series Touring offered a range of gasoline and turbocharged diesel engines, paired with either a 6-speed manual or 8-speed automatic transmission. Rear-wheel drive was standard, with optional all-wheel drive available on select versions.

BMW 3 Series Sedan (F30) LCI

2016-2018

In 2016, BMW unveiled the refreshed version of the fifth-generation 3 Series, featuring subtle exterior tweaks alongside significant technological upgrades.

In 2016, BMW refreshed the fifth generation of its 3 Series to meet Euro 6 emission standards. Instead of calling it a "facelift," they labeled it as an LCI or Life-Cycle Impulse. The exterior got a redesigned front fascia to accommodate radar for Adaptive Cruise Control. Inside, there was a new center console with sliding cup holder covers. The engine lineup was entirely turbocharged to meet emission standards, including a new plug-in hybrid version called the 330e.

Seventh generation

The seventh generation of the BMW 3 Series, launched in 2019, kept its sporty style while getting some modern updates. Inside, it got nicer materials and new tech features. It offered powerful and efficient engines, including turbocharged options for better performance and fuel economy. BMW also added new tech like mild-hybrid systems and improved safety features. Overall, it continued the 3 Series' tradition of blending sportiness, luxury, and innovation.

BMW 3 Series Sedan (G20)

2018-2022

BMW unveiled the seventh generation of the 3 Series in 2018, adding another chapter to its legacy of popular compact executive sedans.

BMW rolled out the G20 generation of the 3 Series in 2018, aiming to uphold its reputation as the compact executive sedan leader. Drawing on cues from its predecessor and the larger 5 Series, it introduced a fresh design on the CLAR platform.

The G20 retained the 3 Series' signature narrow headlights and enlarged kidney grille, with options for bi-xenon or full-LED lights. The front bumper varied based on packages, offering either a sleek or sporty appearance. Its cab-rearward design emphasized the long hood and short rear deck, complemented by full-LED taillights. Inside, digital or analog instrument clusters were available, alongside a touchscreen infotainment system and a center tunnel housing various controls. Seating options included bucket seats for added support during spirited driving.

Engine choices included both diesel and gasoline options, with transmission options of six-speed manual or eight-speed automatic. Most models offered rear- or all-wheel-drive, with a plug-in hybrid variant also available.

BMW 3 Series Touring (G21)

2019-2022

In 2019, BMW unveiled the sixth generation of the 3 Series Touring, marking over three decades and 1.7 million cars on the road. This new station wagon boasts a longer body by 76 mm (3 in), stretching to 4709 mm (15.4 ft), with an extended wheelbase by 41 mm (1.61 in) for added interior space. The trunk capacity has been expanded to 500 liters (17.65 cu-ft), which can triple to 1510 liters (53.3 cu-ft) with the rear seats folded down.

Inside, the 3 Series offers a premium experience with high-quality materials, especially in the upper trim levels. An optional panoramic glass roof enhances the spaciousness and ambiance. The latest generation of BMW's iDrive powers the infotainment system.

Standard LED headlights illuminate the road, with an upgraded LED option and Laserlight available for extra brightness. The Laserlight can extend visibility up to 530 meters.

Under the hood, engine options range from a base four-cylinder turbo diesel with 150 horsepower to the top-tier M340i featuring a 374-horsepower six-cylinder 3.0-liter engine. All-wheel-drive (xDrive) is available for engines producing 190 horsepower and above.

BMW 3-Series Touring

2022-2024

In 2022, BMW refreshed the seventh generation of the 3 Series, upgrading both the sedan and the station wagon.

In 1985, BMW introduced the 3 Series Touring, a station wagon that quickly became popular due to limited premium options in the compact segment.

In the latest update, the car received new, narrower headlights with adaptive LED options and various front bumper designs, including new M Sport packages. The station wagon featured a sportier look with blacked-out B- and C-pillars and a rear diffuser paired with a roof spoiler.

Inside, the dashboard was revamped with the BMW Curved Display, featuring a 12.3" instrument panel and a 14.9" infotainment screen with BMW Operating System 8. The trunk space remained the same as the 2019 model.

Under the hood, there was a range of engines from 148 hp to 369 hp, including gasoline, diesel, and plug-in hybrid options. Power was delivered to the rear or all wheels via a manual or automatic transmission, depending on the engine and options.

BMW 3 Series Sedan

2022-2023

After about three years, BMW refreshed the seventh-generation 3 Series with an update known as the LCI (Life Cycle Impulse).

The BMW 3 Series has been a historic success for the brand, selling over 16 million units across seven generations from 1975 to 2021. The seventh generation, introduced in 2018, continued this trend, with over 1 million units sold by May 2022. Despite this success, BMW refreshed the lineup to maintain momentum.

The updated 3 Series sedan boasted a sportier appearance with redesigned headlights and an enlarged "kidney-grille" at the front, with optional adaptive LED headlamps and M Sport Package variants. Inside, the BMW Curved Display, featuring a 12.3" instrument panel and a 14.9" infotainment screen, elevated the cabin experience.

Under the hood, a variety of engines were available, ranging from 148 hp to 369 hp, including both gasoline and diesel options and a plug-in hybrid variant. Transmission options included manual and automatic, with power distributed to the rear or all corners depending on configuration.

BMW 3 Series Touring

2024-Present

In 2024, BMW took everyone by surprise by giving the 3 Series its second facelift. This update touched every model in the lineup, even the station wagon variant, known as the 3 Series Touring.

In 2018, BMW launched the seventh generation of the 3 Series, known as the G20. Alongside the sedan, they introduced the station wagon variant, called the 3 Series Touring, in June 2019. Traditionally, BMW updated models mid-cycle, with customers expecting a refresh in 2023. However, due to challenges like the pandemic and microchip shortages, BMW adapted, issuing an update, or Life Cycle Impulse (LCI), in May 2022. Surprisingly, just two years later, in May 2024, BMW released another LCI for the 3 Series Touring.

The 2024 update brought new LED headlights with comma-shaped daytime running lights, offering options like adaptive and Matrix-LEDs. The M-Package and M-Package Pro options added sportier touches, like angular grilles and broader air intakes. The car's profile remained sporty, with a distinctive long hood and a sloping roofline that tapered into a raked-forward tailgate.

Inside, BMW installed a curved display, merging the instrument panel and infotainment touchscreen, along with the latest BMW Operating System 8.5. Customizable M-badged steering wheels and various interior trims added personalization options. The split-folding rear bench expanded the trunk space from 500 liters to 1,510 liters.

Engine options included turbocharged gasoline and diesel engines, with a plug-in hybrid 330e version offering impressive EV range. All engines came with an eight-speed automatic transmission with integrated electric motors for hybrid versions, ensuring efficient performance.

BMW 3 Series Sedan

2024-Present

BMW broke tradition with its seventh-generation 3 Series by introducing a second refresh in 2024, a departure from its usual single facelift per generation.

BMW's 3 Series, spanning almost 50 years and over 20 million units sold globally, has evolved from a humble two-door to a diverse lineup including sedans, convertibles, coupes, and wagons. In 2014, BMW split the range into the 3 Series and 4 Series, with the former continuing as sedans and wagons. The seventh generation debuted in 2018, refreshed in 2022, and surprisingly received a second facelift in 2024, termed the Life Cycle Impulse (LCI).

The 2025 model brought significant styling changes, notably redesigned LED headlights and a more angular grille design. Different bumper options were available, including standard, M-Pack, and M-Pack Pro, each with varying air intake designs. The profile featured a long hood and flared wheel arches, while rear designs varied based on trim and engine options.

Inside, a curved display merged the instrument panel and infotainment touchscreen, alongside the introduction of BMW Operating System 8.5. Customization options included M-badged steering wheels and various trim choices. The backseat accommodated three passengers but was constrained by the transmission tunnel.

Engine choices included turbocharged, electrified gasoline and diesel options, with the plug-in hybrid 330e offering an impressive electric-only range. All engines paired with an eight-speed automatic transmission with an integrated electric motor, enhancing efficiency for hybrid versions.
